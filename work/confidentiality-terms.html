confidentiality terms: enhancing readability & user experience

ui/ux designer & integrator

|

6 days

the core goal of this project was to redesign and integrate a significantly updated and expanded privacy policy and create a new protection of personal information page. my solution focused on restructuring dense, complex information into a clear, accessible hierarchy to maintain readability and enhance the user experience.

the problem

the challenge was not that the original confidentiality terms were difficult to read, but rather that a major content update introduced a large volume of new information, making the consolidated document difficult to organize and navigate at first sight. specifically, the updated privacy policy needed to accommodate three major policy categories, which compromised the page's structure and scannability.

figma - prototype of the original privacy policy page

goals

the main goals of this project were to:

  • accommodate new content: successfully integrate a significantly larger volume of updated privacy policy text.

  • enhance structure readability: redesign the page layout to make the complex information easy to read and the page's structure clear.

  • improve information architecture: clearly differentiate between the main policy categories and their numerous sub-sections.

my process

my process involved iterative design focused heavily on optimizing the information architecture for user clarity.

content analysis

i thoroughly reviewed the provided word documents, which outlined the text for the three main policy categories: privacy policy, exo on deman privacy statement, and exo all ears privacy statement.

initial prototype

i created a first figma prototype that use three main accordions for the three categories. the goal was to optimize page length.

figma - prototype of the first version of the privacy policy page

stakeholder feedback & conflict

the project team found the information architecture unclear, as the many sub-sections within each long accordion were hard to differentiate. the team suggested putting accordions inside accordions--a pattern i successfully argued against due to known accessibility and usability issues.

revised prototype

i separated the three policy categories into distinc, labeled sections (using headers) on the page. i then implemented accordions only for the sub-sections within each category. this visually clarified the main structure while optimizing space for detailed content.

figma - prototype of the revised design for the privacy policy page

new page creation

the revised solution was directly applied to the new, simpler protection of personal information page, which was approved immediately due to its clear structure.

figma - prototype of the new protection of personal information page

the solution

the final design provided a clear and accessible solution for navigating complex legal texts:

  • clear sectioning: the three major policy categories were broken out into their own distinct sections, clearly labeled with prominent headers. this immediately established the main information hierarchy.

  • optimized space: accordions were used only for the sub-sections of information within each main category. this ensured that the page remained short, but the user was not forced to expand a single, massive accordion to find what they needed.

  • improved scannability: by separating the main topics, users can quickly and visually identify the category (e.g., "exo on demand") before clicking to reveal the spcific details.

screenshots of the final privacy policy and protection of personal information pages

reflections & learnings

i learned that information architecture is critical for heavy-content pages. when dealing with a large volume of legal or technical text, the risk of a user getting lost is extremely high. in addition to creating a logical structure, it is equally important to use the correct design patterns (like sections vs. nested accordions) to visually reinforce the structure and enhance the content's readability and usability.
